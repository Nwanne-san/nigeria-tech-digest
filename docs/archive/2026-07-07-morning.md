### Headline: Nigeria’s data centres face major test under CBN’s local hosting mandate (TechCabal)

**What happened:** Nigeria's Central Bank (CBN) has issued a sweeping directive mandating banks, fintechs, and payment companies to move all transactional data from overseas cloud servers to data centers located inside Nigeria by January 1, 2027. This policy sets in motion one of the country’s largest cloud migrations to date, shifting critical financial data from global hyperscalers like Amazon Web Services (AWS), Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, and IBM Cloud to domestic infrastructure. The mandate is driven by goals to strengthen data sovereignty, improve regulatory and law-enforcement access during investigations, reduce dependence on foreign jurisdictions, and keep more of the capital Nigerian organizations spend annually on overseas cloud and hosting services within the local economy.

**Deep Lore / Context:** This is a significant **new development in Nigeria's regulatory landscape**, building on previous efforts to assert national control over critical digital infrastructure and aligns with broader global trends of data localization. The CBN's move is driven by concerns over national security, data sovereignty, and economic localization. With electronic transactions surging 80% to a record ₦1.07 quadrillion (approx. $702 billion) in 2024, the volume of financial data is immense, making its local storage a strategic imperative. While Nigerian banks spent a combined ₦177.91 billion (about $116 million) on information technology in the first quarter of 2026, much of this investment still relies on foreign cloud platforms. This policy aims to redirect that spending to the local data center industry, fostering domestic growth and job creation.

The challenge lies in the readiness of Nigeria's data center ecosystem to absorb this massive demand. Industry operators like Ayotunde Coker of Open Access Data Centres (OADC) and Lars Christer Johannisson of Rack Centre claim readiness, citing years of investment and the country's 26 data center facilities (18 commercial) providing between 50 and 56 megawatts (MW) of live computing capacity, making Nigeria Africa's second-largest data center market after South Africa. However, experts like Johnson Agogbua of Kasi Cloud point out that while physical data center capacity might exist, the real need is for more locally available cloud computing and storage platforms—the actual services banks consume—rather than just raw data center space. This mandate could encourage global cloud providers to establish local infrastructure or force financial institutions to adapt to domestic alternatives, posing significant migration challenges for mission-critical payment systems, which involve redesigning applications, validating performance, and ensuring uninterrupted service. This policy will undoubtedly reshape Nigeria's cloud landscape and the tech skills in demand.
