Headline: Tinubu Signs Virtual Assets Executive Order, CBN to Chair New Council

Source: Techpoint Africa

What happened President Bola Tinubu has signed the Presidential Executive Order on Virtual Assets Coordination, 2026, establishing a new framework to bring Nigeria's key financial, security, and revenue agencies under a single umbrella for digital asset oversight. Announced on July 17, 2026, the order takes immediate effect and creates a Virtual Asset Council, to be chaired by the Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN). This council aims to improve coordination among agencies like the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC), Nigeria Revenue Service (NRS), Nigerian Financial Intelligence Unit (NFIU), and the Office of the National Security Adviser (ONSA), addressing regulatory gaps and tackling issues such as fraud, money laundering, terrorism financing, cybersecurity threats, and revenue leakages.

Deep Lore / Context This move marks a significant shift in Nigeria's approach to cryptocurrencies and other virtual assets, following years of regulatory uncertainty and outright restrictions. In 2021, the CBN had controversially barred banks from facilitating cryptocurrency transactions, pushing much of the industry underground. However, there has been a gradual pivot towards regulation rather than outright prohibition, with the SEC introducing licensing programs for Virtual Asset Service Providers (VASPs). This executive order, therefore, follows last week's announcement of the CBN's strategies to stabilize the Naira, which included directives for BDCs and the establishment of a Virtual Asset Council, reflecting a growing recognition that digital assets are too integral to the economy to be ignored. The government's stated goal is to create a clearer regulatory path for legitimate crypto businesses while safeguarding against illicit activities.

The establishment of the Virtual Asset Council, with the CBN at its helm, is a direct response to the "AI Governance and Regulation Gap" storyline we've been tracking, specifically concerning digital asset oversight. Nigeria consistently ranks among the world's leading countries for cryptocurrency adoption, yet regulatory fragmentation has often stifled investment and innovation. The new council is tasked with developing an implementation framework within 30 days. This coordination effort is expected to lay the groundwork for a CBN regulatory sandbox for blockchain and virtual asset products, and a dedicated tax policy from the Nigeria Revenue Service. The Federal Government is also finalizing a Virtual Assets White Paper, which will outline the country's long-term strategy for the industry, aiming to strike a balance between consumer protection and fostering innovation within the "Nigerian tech ecosystem (startups, fintech)" storyline.

Headline: AI Is More Likely Than Humans to Form Biases When Hiring

Source: MIT Tech Review

What happened New research from Princeton University and the University of Chicago suggests that Large Language Models (LLMs) can not only pick up human biases from their training data but also develop their own novel biases from experience, stereotyping job applicants more than humans do. In a simulated hiring game, LLMs like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini quickly segregated candidates from fictional ethnic groups into different jobs based on early, limited observations, even when all candidates were equally likely to succeed. OpenAI’s reasoning model o3 scored 1.83 on a segregation scale (where 2 is maximum), significantly higher than human participants' 0.84. Researchers found that LLMs' eagerness to generalize from limited data, a trait optimized for tasks like math and coding, makes them prone to stereotyping in social contexts. Telling models to be "fair" had little effect, but offering a bonus for diverse hiring significantly reduced bias.

Deep Lore / Context This finding represents a critical escalation in the "AI Governance and Regulation Gap" storyline, particularly concerning ethical AI and AI safety. While the debate has long focused on biases inherited from training data, this research reveals that LLMs can generate new biases through their decision-making processes, even without explicit human instruction. This has profound implications as companies increasingly deploy LLMs for sensitive tasks like screening résumés and conducting interviews, as highlighted in the article. The "AI Security Vulnerabilities" storyline also touches on the need for robust security measures and governance, and this research indicates that "novel biases" are an ever-present vulnerability that requires proactive mitigation.

The study's insights into how LLMs form biases—their tendency to over-generalize from limited data and their optimization for tasks that reward such generalization—are crucial for developers. It suggests that simply "telling" an AI to be fair is insufficient; instead, the design of the AI's goals must incorporate desirable social values. The observation that newer models with higher reasoning capabilities showed stronger biases is particularly concerning, indicating that increased sophistication does not automatically equate to increased fairness. As AI companies race to build agentic models with improved memory and personalization, the risk of these systems forming and acting on their own biases in real-world applications, such as hiring, lending, or parole decisions, becomes a pressing governance challenge that requires immediate attention from policymakers and developers alike.
