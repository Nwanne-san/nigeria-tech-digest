### Reps Direct Accountant-General to Disclose CBN, NNPC Revenue Debts, Probe MDA Fund Withdrawals
**Source:** Premium Times

**What happened**
The House of Representatives Public Accounts Committee (PAC) has issued a directive to the Office of the Accountant-General of the Federation (OAGF), demanding comprehensive records of outstanding revenues allegedly owed to the federal government by key agencies, including the Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) and the Nigerian National Petroleum Company Limited (NNPCL). This action, taken during an investigative hearing on Monday, also sought explanations for allegations that the OAGF withdrew billions of naira from the accounts of several government agencies, such as the Universal Basic Education Commission (UBEC), without prompt refunds. During the session, Director of Revenue and Investment at the OAGF, Makinde Mogaji, disclosed that the CBN allegedly has an outstanding obligation of approximately N5.3 trillion in unremitted operating surplus, despite repeated interventions by the PAC. The Accountant-General of the Federation, Shamseldeen Ogunjimi, defended the practice of withdrawing funds from agency accounts as "temporary borrowing arrangements" to meet urgent financial obligations, insisting that funds were only accessed from unused balances under the Minister of Finance's directives and were eventually refunded. However, Committee Chairman Bamidele Salam rejected this justification, citing complaints from UBEC, NASENI, and NBC that such deductions disrupted their statutory responsibilities, with UBEC alone alleging N16 billion approved expenditure unreleased and N15 billion withdrawn and unrefunded.

**Deep Lore / Context**
This development marks a significant escalation in the ongoing storyline of "Nigeria's Off-Budget Spending Controversy," which has been tracking allegations of N8.83 trillion in off-budget spending and the existence of a potentially fake agency (PFIPC). The current probe by the House PAC directly addresses the lack of transparency and accountability in public finance management, echoing previous concerns about the opaque nature of government spending and revenue remittances. The N5.3 trillion alleged debt from the CBN, if confirmed, represents a substantial portion of the nation's fiscal resources that are not being channeled into the federal treasury, further exacerbating Nigeria's revenue challenges. The OAGF's defense of "temporary borrowing" from statutory agency accounts, even if with ministerial approval, raises serious questions about the legality and propriety of diverting funds specifically appropriated by the National Assembly for critical sectors like education. This practice undermines parliamentary oversight and the autonomy of agencies like UBEC, which are vital for national development, especially given the country's education crisis.

The actors involved here include the legislative arm (House PAC) asserting its oversight function, the executive arm (OAGF, CBN, NNPCL) facing scrutiny, and the public interest (through agencies like UBEC). The incentives for the OAGF to "borrow" from agency accounts likely stem from the federal government's persistent revenue shortfalls and the pressure to meet urgent financial obligations. However, this approach risks creating a parallel, less transparent financial system that bypasses established budgetary processes. The NNPCL's alleged non-cooperation with the recovery process further highlights the challenges in enforcing fiscal discipline among powerful state-owned enterprises. This probe is crucial because it touches upon the very foundation of fiscal governance, transparency, and the rule of law. The outcome could either reinforce legislative authority and improve financial accountability or, if unresolved, perpetuate the cycle of revenue leakage and undermine public trust in government institutions, directly impacting the nation's economic stability and ability to fund essential services.

### Tinubu Asks Reps to Fast-Track State Police Bill
**Source:** Premium Times

**What happened**
President Bola Tinubu has formally requested the House of Representatives to expedite consideration of a constitutional amendment bill aimed at establishing state police. In a letter dated June 15 and addressed to Speaker Abbas Tajudeen, President Tinubu described the Constitution of the Federal Republic of Nigeria (Alteration) (State Police) Bill, 2026, as a "critical component" of his administration's strategy to strengthen national security. He urged lawmakers to give the bill prompt attention, noting that it builds on previous legislative efforts by the House while incorporating "additional safeguards" to ensure an effective dual policing structure. Speaker Tajudeen Abbas confirmed that the House would withdraw its earlier version of the State Police Bill to consider this more comprehensive executive proposal, developed by a presidential committee chaired by the Chief of Staff to the President, Femi Gbajabiamila. The Speaker assured Nigerians that the proposed legislation would undergo public hearings and scrutiny before passage.

**Deep Lore / Context**
This presidential push significantly escalates the "State Police Debate and Implementation Challenges" storyline, which has been developing with governors like Lawal asserting the need for state police to strengthen their roles in tackling insecurity. The move signals a strong executive commitment to decentralizing Nigeria's policing system, a long-standing and contentious issue since the return to democratic rule in 1999. Under the current constitutional arrangement, policing is exclusively vested in the federally controlled Nigeria Police Force. However, worsening insecurity—including terrorism, banditry, kidnapping, and communal clashes—has fueled persistent calls for decentralization, with many arguing that the centralized structure is overstretched and ineffective. The establishment of regional security outfits like Amotekun and Ebube Agu, despite lacking constitutional policing powers, underscores the urgent demand for localized security responses.

The debate around state police has historically been fraught with concerns about potential abuse by state governors, politicization, and funding challenges. The "additional safeguards" mentioned by President Tinubu are crucial in addressing these fears, aiming to ensure that a dual policing structure operates effectively without undermining national cohesion or becoming a tool for political oppression. The fact that the executive has now formally transmitted a bill, and the House Speaker has committed to prioritizing it, suggests a concerted effort to overcome previous legislative hurdles. For the proposal to become law, it requires approval from at least two-thirds of both chambers of the National Assembly, followed by ratification by no fewer than 24 of the 36 state Houses of Assembly. This process highlights the significant political will and broad consensus needed to enact such a fundamental constitutional change, which could profoundly reshape Nigeria's security architecture and its federal system.
