The PAC's direct query to the AGF and the demand for detailed records signal a more aggressive stance by the legislature to enforce fiscal discipline and transparency, moving beyond mere rhetoric to concrete demands for financial documentation. ### Experts Warn Nigeria Against 'New Oil Curse' Amid Critical Minerals Rush
**What happened:** A coalition of environmental experts, traditional leaders, and academics has issued a stark warning that Nigeria risks falling into a "new resource curse" if it fails to radically reform the governance of its rapidly expanding solid minerals sector. This caution was delivered at the Third Nigeria Socio-Ecological Alternatives Convergence (NSAC) in Abuja, where participants highlighted that the global demand for critical minerals—such as lithium, rare earth elements, gold, copper, and platinum group metals—could replicate the environmental devastation, conflict, and inequality seen in the Niger Delta's oil exploitation. The warning comes after the Federal Government's announcement of a "world-class polymetallic mineral province" in Kaduna State, aiming to position Nigeria as a key supplier for electric vehicle batteries and renewable energy technologies. Professor Omolade Adunbi of the University of Michigan emphasized that critical minerals are becoming "the new oil" and could reproduce "dispossession, deforestation, insecurity, elite capture and ecological degradation" if governed by the same extractive logic. Environmentalist Nnimmo Bassey questioned the excitement over new discoveries without addressing past governance failures, noting Nigeria's forests are disappearing at an alarming rate of 250,000 to 300,000 hectares annually, potentially leading to their complete loss by 2052. The Emir of Nasarawa, Alhaji Ibrahim Usman Jibril, linked illegal mining and deforestation to rising banditry and organized crime, with criminal groups financing operations through illegal mining and timber trafficking.

**Deep Lore / Context:** This story is profoundly significant, touching upon Nigeria's long-term economic strategy, environmental sustainability, and national security, while also providing context for the "Nigerian tech ecosystem" and "AI Stock Market Exuberance and Caution" storylines by highlighting the raw material demand. Nigeria's history with crude oil has been a classic example of the "resource curse," where abundant natural wealth paradoxically leads to economic stagnation, corruption, and conflict. The Niger Delta, once a vibrant ecosystem, has been ravaged by oil spills, gas flaring, and community disenfranchisement, fueling militancy and insecurity. The current rush for critical minerals, driven by the global energy transition and the burgeoning demand for components in AI, electric vehicles, and advanced electronics, presents both an immense opportunity and a grave danger. The Federal Government's ambition to become a leading supplier is understandable given the economic potential, but the warnings from experts are a crucial reminder of past mistakes.

The "Just Minerals Strategy" proposed by Professor Adunbi, advocating for legally binding community consent, prohibition of mining in ecologically sensitive areas, environmental restoration, and fair benefit sharing, is a direct response to the failures of the Petroleum Industry Act (PIA) and previous resource management policies. The link between environmental degradation (deforestation) and insecurity, as highlighted by Nnimmo Bassey and Emir Jibril, is particularly alarming. Forests stripped by illegal logging and mining become operational bases and safe havens for bandits and kidnappers, exacerbating the "Nigeria's Infrastructure for Insecurity Response" challenge. This creates a vicious cycle: demand for minerals drives illegal mining, which fuels deforestation and insecurity, further destabilizing communities and hindering legitimate economic development. For Nigeria to avoid repeating its oil curse, it must establish a robust, transparent, and community-centric governance framework for its solid minerals sector. This means prioritizing environmental justice, securing community rights, and ensuring that the economic benefits are equitably distributed and contribute to sustainable development, rather than enriching a few and impoverishing many. The challenge lies in overcoming entrenched corruption and political patronage that have historically enabled illegal exploitation and undermined regulatory efforts.
