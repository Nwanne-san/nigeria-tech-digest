### CBN’s Data Reveals Wide Disparity in Bank Lending Rates
**Source:** BusinessDay

**What happened:** The Central Bank of Nigeria's (CBN) latest disclosure of commercial and merchant banks' lending rates shows a significant variation in the cost of borrowing across the banking industry. Prime lending rates range from as low as 19.5 percent to over 40 percent, depending on the lender and the borrower's credit profile. Guaranty Trust Bank (GTBank) posted the lowest prime lending rate at 21.0 percent, while Tatum Bank recorded the highest prime rate at 41.65 percent. Stanbic IBTC reported the widest gap, with a prime lending rate of 1.0 percent and a maximum lending rate of 60.0 percent. These figures were published on July 3, ahead of next week's Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) meeting, where analysts expect the benchmark Monetary Policy Rate (MPR) to remain unchanged at 26.5 percent.

**Deep Lore / Context:** This report provides crucial, granular data that directly feeds into the "CBN policy" storyline, illustrating the real-world impact of monetary policy on businesses and individuals. While the CBN reduced the MPR by 50 basis points in February, the latest data indicate that borrowing costs remain elevated and highly variable. This wide disparity in lending rates underscores the challenges faced by many Nigerian businesses, particularly SMEs, in accessing affordable credit, which is vital for economic growth and job creation. The CBN's publication, part of its consumer protection and transparency framework, aims to provide greater visibility into how banks price loans, but the figures themselves highlight a market where risk assessment and operational costs lead to vastly different financing conditions.

The context here is Nigeria's persistent struggle with high inflation and the CBN's tight monetary policy stance, which has kept the MPR high. Standard Chartered's forecast of a 25% MPR by year-end 2026, as noted in previous briefings, suggests that high interest rates are likely to persist. Banks determine their lending rates based on factors such as their cost of raising deposits, operating expenses, liquidity, expected inflation, and the perceived risk of each borrower. The unusually low prime rate from Stanbic IBTC (1.0%) suggests it might apply to a very specific, limited category of facilities or customers, rather than its general lending portfolio, further complicating the picture for average borrowers. The upcoming MPC meeting will be closely watched, as a decision to hold rates, influenced by renewed tensions in the Middle East pushing up crude oil prices, would signal a continued focus on containing inflation, but also mean sustained high borrowing costs for the foreseeable future, potentially stifling investment and economic expansion.
