The "mounting financial pressure" and "prolonged funding slowdown" mentioned in the articles are critical factors. FoodCourt's struggles highlight the need for robust financial discipline, realistic growth projections, and diversified funding strategies in a market characterized by rising operating costs, weaker consumer spending, and high inflation. ### 2. Nigeria Expands Its Crypto Sandbox, Admits Nine More Companies
**Source:** TechCabal, Techpoint Africa
**What happened**
Nigeria's Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) has significantly expanded its Accelerated Regulatory Incubation Programme (ARIP), a regulatory sandbox for virtual asset companies, by admitting nine additional firms. This move follows the initial admission of Busha and Quidax in June 2024, after the Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) lifted its 2021 ban on banks working with crypto firms in December 2023. The new batch, admitted on July 2 and 3, includes Luno, GetEquity, Koinkoin, WrappedCBDC, Trovotech, Blockvault, Bitbarter, GIGX Technologies, and KuCoin. SEC Director-General Emomotimi Agama noted in October 2025 that while millions of Nigerians take financial risks through gambling and cryptocurrencies, fewer than three million invest in the capital market, indicating an "appetite for risk" that ARIP aims to channel into the regulated sector.

**Deep Lore / Context**
Nigeria's journey with cryptocurrency regulation has been characterized by a cautious, often oscillating, approach. The initial ban in 2021 by the CBN reflected concerns about financial stability, money laundering, and consumer protection in an unregulated market. However, the subsequent lifting of the ban in December 2023 and the expansion of the SEC's ARIP signal a significant policy shift towards embracing, rather than outright rejecting, the burgeoning crypto industry. This change is driven by the undeniable reality of Nigeria's massive crypto market, which received approximately $92.1 billion in crypto value between July 2024 and June 2025, making it one of the world's largest. Regulators recognize that an outright ban merely pushes activity into informal, untraceable channels, posing greater risks.

The ARIP serves as a critical "learner's permit" for crypto companies, allowing them to operate in a supervised environment while the SEC evaluates their compliance with anti-money laundering rules, customer fund management, and operational risks. This phased approach reflects the dynamic nature of the digital asset industry, where new products and business models emerge rapidly, making traditional, rigid licensing frameworks difficult to apply. By using a sandbox, the SEC aims to strike a balance between fostering innovation and ensuring consumer protection and financial stability. The challenge, as highlighted by the nearly two-year wait for Busha and Quidax to graduate, is whether the regulatory pipeline can move quickly enough to keep pace with the market's rapid evolution, or if it will create bottlenecks that stifle growth. The current expansion suggests an accelerated intent to formalize the sector, which is a positive signal for the long-term health and legitimacy of crypto operations in Nigeria.
