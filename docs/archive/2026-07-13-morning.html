FG Suspends Planned WAEC, NECO Fee Hike

Source: Daily Trust, Channels TV, Nairametrics, Vanguard

What happened: The Federal Government has suspended its proposed review of registration fees for the 2027 West African Senior School Certificate Examination (WASSCE) and the National Examinations Council (NECO) Senior School Certificate Examination (SSCE). The decision, announced by the Federal Ministry of Education, withdraws its June 18, 2026, letter that had conveyed an 82% increase, raising the fee from N27,500 to N50,000 per candidate. Minister of Education, Dr. Maruf Tunji Alausa, directed the proposal be put on hold, citing "concerns and feedback from members of the public" and a commitment to "inclusive, transparent and evidence-based policymaking." The ministry stated that while the proposed review was necessitated by rising operational costs associated with conducting credible national examinations, the fee increase will not take effect until a comprehensive review and extensive consultations with examination bodies, state ministries of education, school proprietors, parents' associations, organized labor, and other stakeholders are concluded.

Deep Lore / Context: This suspension marks a significant and swift response to widespread public outcry and criticism, directly impacting the ongoing storyline of "Peter Obi's Political Pressure on Tinubu." Opposition figures, including Atiku Abubakar, had vehemently critiqued the initial fee hike, describing it as "cruel and economically insensitive" and fundamentally incompatible with the government's constitutional responsibilities. The proposed N50,000 fee, representing an 82% increase, had generated fears among education experts and parents that it would place additional financial pressure on millions of struggling families, potentially worsening the already dire out-of-school crisis. The government's quick reversal, framed as a move towards "inclusive, transparent and evidence-based policymaking," suggests an attempt to mitigate political fallout and address public discontent, demonstrating a reactive sensitivity to public sentiment on critical issues like education affordability.

The context of rising operational costs for examination bodies, encompassing logistics, security, printing of examination materials, technology deployment, supervision, and general administration, has been a recurring justification for periodic fee adjustments. However, the timing of such a sharp increase, amidst high inflation, declining purchasing power, and other economic hardships, amplified public resistance. The initial approval by the Federal Ministry of Education, following a meeting with examination bodies on March 31, 2026, aimed for a uniform fee across WAEC and NECO, but the lack of prior comprehensive public consultation proved to be a major misstep. This episode highlights the delicate balance the government must strike between funding essential public services and ensuring equitable access, especially in a sector as sensitive and politically charged as education, which remains a key battleground for political narratives and public trust.
