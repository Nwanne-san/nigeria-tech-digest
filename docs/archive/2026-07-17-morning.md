**1. CBN Mandates BDCs to Sell Unutilised FX to NFEM Within 24hrs / Nigeria’s Central Bank to Track Every Retail Dollar**
**Source:** Vanguard, Techpoint Africa
**What happened:** The Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) has issued a new directive mandating Bureau De Change (BDC) operators to sell any unutilised foreign exchange (FX) balances to the Nigerian Foreign Exchange Market (NFEM) within 24 hours of the expiry of the utilisation period. Failure to comply will result in regulatory sanctions, including forfeiture of the unutilised balance and suspension of the BDC's NFEM access. This is part of a broader strategy by the CBN to track every retail dollar entering the country, requiring banks and authorised dealers to provide more detailed reporting on retail FX transactions, including recipient information, usage, and eventual destination of funds. The CBN will maintain a centralised portal, the FX BDC Purchase Tracker (FXBT), for real-time data submission and oversight.

**Deep Lore / Context:** This is a significant escalation in the **CBN policy** storyline, which has been tracking the apex bank's aggressive strategies to stabilize the Naira and boost foreign exchange inflows. The previous digest noted the CBN's introduction of a BDC FX tracker and 24-hour resale mandate, alongside a target for increased diaspora remittances. This new directive reinforces and expands upon those measures, demonstrating the CBN's determination to exert tighter control over the FX market. The explicit threat of forfeiture and suspension signals a zero-tolerance approach to hoarding or speculative practices by BDCs, which have historically contributed to market volatility and the widening gap between official and parallel market rates.

The move to "track every retail dollar" through enhanced reporting and the FXBT portal is aimed at improving transparency, curbing round-tripping, and detecting suspicious transactions. This builds on earlier reforms, such as dismantling multiple exchange-rate windows and introducing AI-powered transaction monitoring for banks in March 2026. For the CBN, better data visibility is crucial for understanding demand patterns, shaping future policy, and ensuring that scarce FX resources are directed towards legitimate economic activities rather than speculation. This aggressive regulatory stance, while potentially bringing stability, also places a significant compliance burden on BDCs and authorised dealers, and its effectiveness will depend on robust enforcement and the capacity of the FXBT system to handle real-time data from numerous operators.
