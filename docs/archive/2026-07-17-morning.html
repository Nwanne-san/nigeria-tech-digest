FG to Track Poverty, Incomes to Assess Impact of Reforms — Oyedele Source: Vanguard What happened: The Minister of Finance and Coordinating Minister of the Economy, Taiwo Oyedele, announced the Federal Government's plan to publish indicators tracking poverty, incomes, and inequality. This initiative aims to assess the real-world impact of the government's economic reforms on the living standards of Nigerians. Speaking at a BusinessDay conference in Lagos, Oyedele stated that the government would measure "shared prosperity" through three metrics: reductions in multidimensional poverty, increases in real income per capita, and lower inequality. Deep Lore / Context: This development is a direct response to the escalating public discourse around the efficacy and human cost of the current administration's economic reforms, particularly the removal of fuel subsidies and the liberalization of the naira. The government is under immense pressure to demonstrate that the sacrifices demanded of citizens are yielding broader, equitable gains, especially amidst persistent concerns over fiscal transparency, which ties into the Nigeria's Off-Budget Spending Controversy storyline. The Senate President's recent admission of not seeing the National Assembly's budget has intensified scrutiny, making the government's commitment to publishing poverty indicators a critical step towards rebuilding public trust and accountability.

Oyedele's acknowledgment that "A stable economy can still be a stagnant one if we become complacent" underscores the government's awareness of the disconnect between macroeconomic stability and household welfare. The challenge lies not just in collecting and publishing data, but in ensuring its accuracy, accessibility, and responsiveness to policy adjustments. The lack of a specific timeline for publishing these indicators or their frequency leaves room for skepticism, but the stated intent signals a shift towards a more data-driven approach to social impact assessment. This initiative could potentially provide a clearer picture of where reforms are succeeding and where more targeted interventions are needed, offering a crucial feedback loop for policy adjustments in the coming months.

1. CBN Mandates BDCs to Sell Unutilised FX to NFEM Within 24hrs / Nigeria’s Central Bank to Track Every Retail Dollar Source: Vanguard, Techpoint Africa What happened: The Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) has issued a new directive mandating Bureau De Change (BDC) operators to sell any unutilised foreign exchange (FX) balances to the Nigerian Foreign Exchange Market (NFEM) within 24 hours of the expiry of the utilisation period. Failure to comply will result in regulatory sanctions, including forfeiture of the unutilised balance and suspension of the BDC's NFEM access. This is part of a broader strategy by the CBN to track every retail dollar entering the country, requiring banks and authorised dealers to provide more detailed reporting on retail FX transactions, including recipient information, usage, and eventual destination of funds. The CBN will maintain a centralised portal, the FX BDC Purchase Tracker (FXBT), for real-time data submission and oversight.

Deep Lore / Context: This is a significant escalation in the CBN policy storyline, which has been tracking the apex bank's aggressive strategies to stabilize the Naira and boost foreign exchange inflows. The previous digest noted the CBN's introduction of a BDC FX tracker and 24-hour resale mandate, alongside a target for increased diaspora remittances. This new directive reinforces and expands upon those measures, demonstrating the CBN's determination to exert tighter control over the FX market. The explicit threat of forfeiture and suspension signals a zero-tolerance approach to hoarding or speculative practices by BDCs, which have historically contributed to market volatility and the widening gap between official and parallel market rates.

The move to "track every retail dollar" through enhanced reporting and the FXBT portal is aimed at improving transparency, curbing round-tripping, and detecting suspicious transactions. This builds on earlier reforms, such as dismantling multiple exchange-rate windows and introducing AI-powered transaction monitoring for banks in March 2026. For the CBN, better data visibility is crucial for understanding demand patterns, shaping future policy, and ensuring that scarce FX resources are directed towards legitimate economic activities rather than speculation. This aggressive regulatory stance, while potentially bringing stability, also places a significant compliance burden on BDCs and authorised dealers, and its effectiveness will depend on robust enforcement and the capacity of the FXBT system to handle real-time data from numerous operators.
